"Hrrmmm... humm...", muttered the wizard, as he leafed erratically through a gigantic, dusty old leather bound tome that looked like it had an axe dent in the middle of its back cover, the pages like the sound of dry leaves scraping across cobblestone. His beard, spindled with his long dull white locks, and mottled with grey and silver, brushed and settled on the bottom half of the pages as they were sporadically turned. "Ah, yes, here it is! Recipe for rubenstia! It looks as if...". And his words trailed off as he realised where he was. Again. At the front of your class at The Helm, School of Programming Wizardry, a class that has grown quite accustomed to these sorts of divergences from the Lesson Plan (if such a thing existed). As if to punctuate the student's boredom and lack of clear direction during these moments, there was the sound of shoes shuffling quickly to attention, and books being closed as Old Kellen squinted his gaze around the room. "Where was I? Oh, yes! Chai... how to get Mocha and Chai set up in a jiffy for testing your code. Alright, class, turn to page LXXXVI in your Wizard Tools Handbook, and we'll dig right in..."

It's been three months since you started your program at The Helm, and as an Apprentice Wizard, you feel somewhat at a loss for clear enough words to describe the feeling that perhaps you made the wrong decision. Your teachers are kindly, and seem to have an enormous amount of wisdom when speaking on the topic of the Arcane Arts. Any other time, however, their understanding of how your mind works and how you feel overwhelmed by the sheer volume of things you know nothing about seems to be as mysterious to them as the incantations they recite from memory that make the air almost spark into flame with electric energy.

Are you struggling for motivation to stay in our program? Feeling lost under a huge pile of new terms and concepts? Never fear! You've taken on an enormous task, and sometimes it's even difficult for our seasoned Wizards to know how to best explain something so that the entire class (including you) can understand it.
